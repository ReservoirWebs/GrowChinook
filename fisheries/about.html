This linked model uses Python 3 and the NumPy and SciPy packages (www.python.org) with the formulas from the Wisconsin Bioenergetics model (Hanson et al. 1997) and the visual encounter foraging model for salmonids (Beauchamp et al.1999) linked using foraging to limit the proportion (P) of maximum consumption (Cmax), when foraging is lower (Figure 1).

From the Wisconsin model, where ƒ(T) is a temperature dependence function:
Growth = Consumption - (Respiration + Waste)
Consumption = Cmax * P * ƒ(T)

From the visual encounter foraging model:
Encounter rate = Search Volume * Prey Density

To link the models:
P = minimum of (encounter rate / Cmax) or 1
Refer to Table 1 for a full list of parameter values used. The model has been modified to accommodate vertical profile inputs, day and night light levels, and diel vertical migration by Chinook Salmon juveniles. It has also been structured to allow for user determined restrictions in depth or temperatures.

By default, the model seeks to achieve maximum growth for a single juvenile Chinook Salmon given the input conditions. The optimization routine compares 0.5 meter intervals and determines the maximum growth which can be achieved at day and night periods before compounding daily growth.

Input data were collected from 2013-2016 from 3 reservoirs in the upper Willamette Basin, Oregon (Fall Creek Reservoir, 43°56'33"N 122°45'25"W, Hills Creek Reservoir, 43°40'16"N 122°25'33"W, and Lookout Point Reservoir, 43°53'48"N 122°43'34"W; Figure 2). Collections included light, temperature, zooplankton size, composition, and distribution. Light was collected using a LiCor Li-192 sensor. Extinction coefficients were calculated using the exponential decay curve fits of light for each reservoir and month. Zooplankton vertical distribution was collected at 2 m depths using a 5L transparent Van Dorn sampler where samples were enumerated and identified to species. Zooplankton size and abundance was calculated using a 0.5 m diameter vertically integrated 64 µm tow net, where samples were identified to species and length measurements were collected for a subset of each species.

Table 1. Parameter values used in the model backend and their source.

Value Source Notes
Chinook Salmon adult bioenergetics parameters Stewart and Ibarra 1991
Daytime surface irradiance (lux) Field data from LiCor Li-192 sensor (converted to lux using conversion factor of 54) Ranges from average 24800 lux in shade to average 53900 lux in full sun, used 39350 in model
Nighttime surface irradiance (lux) Kyba et al. 2017 Authors recommend 0.05-0.10 lux for physiological studies
Juvenile Chinook Salmon swimming speed Celedonia et al. 2008 Milling speed, not Ucrit – Used the daytime mean swim velocity observed in Lake Washington (2.0 body lengths / second)
Juvenile Chinook Salmon weight to fork length conversion MacFarlane and Norton 2008
Daphnia weight Holeck et al. 2011 Ghazy et al. 2009 Used wet weight to dry weight conversion
Daphnia digestibility Noüe and Choubert 1985
Daphnia energy density Luecke and Brandt 1993 22.7 kJ / g
Change in condition Uses the amount of loss over the fish length to calculate condition loss
